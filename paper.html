BotGarden: A Multi-Asset Laboratory for Reproducible Paper Trading

A practical architecture for bounded stock, option, and cryptocurrency strategy generation, historical screening, explainable scheduled execution, and human-directed evolutionary experimentation.

Technical report21 July 2026Version 1.1Open web implementation
Abstract

BotGarden is a multi-user web laboratory for constructing, screening, and operating rule-based stock, equity-option, and cryptocurrency strategies through individual Alpaca paper accounts. The system combines family-aware randomized generation, composable start conditions, bounded child mutation, stock and crypto simulation, explicitly uncertain option replay estimates, five-minute scheduled execution, portfolio-aware risk gates, live account and position reporting, market-close controls, and per-cycle explanations. Its objective is not automated discovery of guaranteed profit; it is a reproducible environment in which heterogeneous strategies can be generated, compared, mutated, observed, and rejected without deploying real capital. This report specifies the architecture, strategy representation, evaluation model, execution controls, security boundary, and principal threats to validity.

Keywords: algorithmic trading, paper trading, strategy generation, backtesting, options, cryptocurrency, DCA, grid trading, explainability, Alpaca API, Supabase

1. Introduction

Retail trading platforms commonly separate strategy construction, historical evaluation, and broker execution into different products. BotGarden investigates whether these activities can be joined in a lightweight browser portal while retaining transparent assumptions and user control across securities and continuous cryptocurrency markets. A signed-in user connects an individual Alpaca paper account, defines or generates bots, evaluates them over recent sessions, observes live equity, exposure, positions, and profit or loss, and decides which configurations remain enabled.

The research premise is modest: systematic experimentation is more useful when the experiment is reproducible, bounded, and observable. Randomness alone is not a strategy. Accordingly, BotGarden first selects a coherent strategy family and then varies parameters within family-specific constraints. The resulting configuration, including every randomized field and a generation identifier, is stored with the bot. Parent strategies may produce bounded child variants that are evaluated over the same historical window.

The client is a no-build progressive web interface hosted as static assets. Authentication, relational state, and row-level authorization are provided by Supabase. Server-side TypeScript functions validate credentials, retrieve broker data, run historical evaluations, select liquid symbols, and submit paper orders. Supabase documents Edge Functions as globally distributed, Deno-compatible TypeScript services intended for authenticated integrations with third-party APIs [1].

A Postgres cron job invokes the execution worker every five minutes. Supabase's documented scheduling pattern combines pg_cron, asynchronous HTTP, and secrets stored in Vault [2]. BotGarden uses idempotent client-order identifiers, existing-exposure checks, and latest-cycle status replacement to reduce duplicate submissions and make the reason for each action or non-action visible.

3. Strategy Representation and Generation

3.1 Entry conditions

Each bot stores one to three structured conditions joined by AND or OR. Supported families include price and percentage movement, volume, relative volume, RSI, VWAP, moving-average relationships, Bollinger Bands, ATR, MACD, opening-range breaks, and gaps. Random bots use a 15%/60%/25% distribution for one, two, and three conditions, respectively. A one-condition variant retains the primary signal; two-condition variants add confirmation; three-condition variants add a compatible trend, volume, or momentum filter.

3.2 Bounded stock generation

Stock templates represent pullback, trend-continuation, breakout, mean-reversion, and experimental gap behavior. Template selection is weighted, after which risk posture and time horizon constrain parameter ranges. Profit targets, stops, and averaging distances share a generated volatility assumption rather than being sampled independently. Averaging is limited to one to three additions with mild order growth, and cumulative spacing is constrained to remain inside the protective-stop boundary.

3.3 Bounded option generation

Option generation supports credit spreads, debit spreads, and long calls or puts, while favoring defined-risk credit structures. Credit-spread defaults emphasize moderate expirations, relatively low short-option delta, early profit realization, expiration avoidance, and a capped loss multiple. The bulk generator defaults to a 60% credit-spread, 20% debit-spread, and 20% long-option mixture. Multi-leg orders are represented atomically; Alpaca describes an MLeg order as a combined order containing multiple contracts on the same underlying and documents the order_class: mleg request structure [3].

3.4 Parent–child experiments

A child inherits its parent's ticker, strategy family, risk envelope, and test duration while receiving a new generation identifier and bounded parameter mutations. This matched-window design makes the immediate comparison more interpretable than testing parent and child across different market samples. It does not, however, eliminate selection bias or multiple-testing risk.

3.5 Cryptocurrency strategy families

The cryptocurrency workspace separates continuous-market strategies from exchange-session-dependent securities. Its randomized batches draw from safety-order DCA, volatility-adaptive grid, volatility momentum, volatility accumulation, shock recovery, smart trailing, and regime-allocation families. Family weights and parameter ranges favor coherent configurations rather than assuming that any one family is intrinsically profitable. Volatility-scaled spacing, fee-aware exits, trailing protection, break-even behavior, maximum holding periods, spread checks, exposure limits, and drawdown gates form a shared risk layer. Crypto parents can create bounded children for matched-period comparison.

4. Historical Evaluation

4.1 Stock simulation

Stock backtests consume adjusted Alpaca bars, evaluate conditions on completed candles, simulate entries and averaging orders, and close positions at configured profit, loss, or end-of-test rules. Results include return, net profit or loss, signal and trade counts, maximum drawdown, daily market classification, and an underlying-price path. The free Alpaca market-data offering uses IEX rather than the consolidated SIP feed [4]; therefore, results can differ from a full-market reconstruction.

Rbot = (Cend − C0) / C0 × 100

Where C0 is configured test capital and Cend is simulated ending capital after forced closure of any residual position. BotGarden aggregates repeated tests using capital-weighted profit, but also retains each run and its market context.

4.2 Option replay estimation

Historical option-chain and Greek snapshots are not reconstructed by the current system. Instead, BotGarden evaluates entry signals on the underlying and produces a low-confidence estimate using underlying movement, configured delta, premium, spread width, bounded payoff, modeled time decay, exit rules, and a liquidity haircut. Estimated return is visually and semantically separated from actual stock backtest return and from future observed paper P&L.

P̂ = f(Δ·ΔS, θ·Δt, premium, width, liquidity, exit rule), subject to strategy payoff bounds

This estimator is intended for coarse pruning, not valuation. It omits historical implied-volatility surfaces, changing Greeks, assignment dynamics, quote-level spreads, and execution priority. Consequently, it must not be interpreted as evidence that an option strategy would have achieved the displayed result.

4.3 Regime context and visualization

Each run labels daily direction, path efficiency, and intraday range-based volatility. Compact row sparklines show what the underlying did during the latest test, usually five market days. These contextual features help distinguish a broadly weak bot from a bot tested outside its intended regime, although a five-day sample remains statistically small.

4.4 Cryptocurrency replay

Crypto replay applies each family’s entry, accumulation, and exit logic to historical bars while accounting for configured fees and bounded risk controls. Because cryptocurrencies trade continuously, elapsed calendar time and session-independent exits replace stock-market session assumptions. Replay remains a bar-based simulation: intrabar path, quote spread, partial fills, latency, and adverse selection can differ from paper execution.

5. Paper Execution and Observability

Enabled bots are evaluated on a five-minute schedule during eligible market sessions. Before entry, the worker verifies conditions, exposure, open orders, risk, option-chain availability, contract structure, premium, and liquidity. Each bot receives a latest-cycle explanation indicating whether conditions passed, an order was submitted, exposure blocked entry, or another safety gate intervened.

For supported option positions, related legs are monitored as one strategy and may be closed together at the configured profit target, loss threshold, or exit DTE. Crypto bots use the shared profit-protection and risk layer; newly generated specialized stock strategies can carry an analogous exit policy. The Activity workspace separates stocks, options, and crypto, displays live position quantity, entry price, current price, market value, and unrealized profit or loss, and permits user-directed market closure. Securities can be closed only while the stock market is open; cryptocurrency positions can be closed continuously.

The Overview is deliberately account-centric rather than bot-centric. It retrieves live Alpaca paper equity, cash, buying power, open-position count, unrealized profit or loss, daily equity change, and exposure by asset class. Individual configuration details remain in the relevant stock/options or crypto workspace. Alpaca states that its paper environment uses the same API specification as live trading but remains a simulation and does not fully model market impact, latency slippage, information leakage, or queue position [5]. BotGarden therefore treats paper results as operational evidence, not a live-performance guarantee.

6. Security and Multi-User Isolation

Broker credentials are submitted to an authenticated server-side function, encrypted with AES-GCM, and never returned to the browser. User-owned tables employ row-level security. The scheduled worker accesses records with elevated service credentials but reestablishes ownership boundaries explicitly in its queries and status writes. Secrets used for encryption and scheduling are environment-managed rather than committed to the repository.

The primary threat classes are credential disclosure, cross-user data access, duplicate order submission, and overbroad scheduled execution. Mitigations include encryption at rest, RLS, user-scoped broker connections, deterministic cycle identifiers, exposure checks, and audit events. These controls reduce risk but do not constitute a formal security proof.

7. Decision Support and Pruning

Bots are ranked within their asset workspace by stock or crypto backtest return and, separately, by estimated option replay return. Workspace-specific bulk actions can remove configurations below 2%, including untested bots and option bots lacking an estimate, without pruning the other asset workspace. This rule is convenient but statistically naive. A more defensible future rule should require minimum coverage and trade count, penalize uncertainty and drawdown, and evaluate an unseen walk-forward interval.

Score = return − λd(drawdown) − λu(uncertainty) + λc(consistency)

The current product should therefore be viewed as an experiment manager rather than an optimizer. Generating many bots and retaining only recent winners creates multiple-comparison and survivorship bias. Parent–child generation makes experimentation easier, but also increases the need for holdout periods and family-level reporting.

9. Future Work

The immediate roadmap comprises broader stock exit-policy coverage, global daily-loss limits, correlation-aware capital allocation, minimum-evidence pruning, walk-forward validation, and regime-specific scorecards. A shadow mode would allow signal collection without order submission. Longer-term work should replace option replay estimates with licensed historical quote or chain data, synchronize broker fills into realized strategy-level performance, preserve longitudinal account-equity snapshots, and compare parent–child families using preregistered holdout windows.

10. Conclusion

BotGarden demonstrates a compact multi-asset architecture for moving from structured strategy ideas to reproducible paper experiments and observable portfolio operation. Its contribution is chiefly organizational: coherent family-aware random generation, explicit assumptions, matched child experiments, broker-connected paper execution, shared risk layers, live account and position visibility, direct position closure, and a visible explanation for each scheduled decision. The system does not solve the prediction problem, and it intentionally makes no claim of profitable performance. Its value depends on disciplined evaluation, conservative risk controls, and continued separation of modeled, simulated, paper-observed, and live outcomes.
